Soy Commodity Chain Analysis

About This Project

Soybeans have quickly become one of the most important staple crops of the world. First cultivated in ancient East Asia, these oilseeds are now widely grown in many countries across the world, and soy is a component of countless food products. Our aim in this project is to examine multiple facets of the soy commodity chain in order to investigate the factors behind the rise of the soybean in recent decades. We have chosen four main angles for our investigation: geography, labor, economics, and ecology.

Where Does Soy Go?


Soy has become one of the most widely grown commodity crops - but how is it consumed?

Soybeans


Soybeans can be processed and used whole, but are primarily crushed to separate the bean into oil and protein-rich meal.

Whole Beans


Whole soybeans are sometimes processed into full-fat soy flour. Whole beans or full-fat soy flour is used in products like soymilk, tofu, and soy sauce. Immature beans may also be cooked and eaten.



Soybean Meal


Soybean meal is rich in protein, and an excellent animal feed. About 85% of the global soybean crop is processed into soybean meal and oil, and the bulk of the resulting meal is fed to livestock. Soybean meal can also be further processed into defatted soy flour.

Soybean Oil


Soybean oil is one of the top vegetable oils and ubiquitous in processed foods. It can also be processed into lecithin, a common emulsifier, among numerous other products.

Synthesis

The exploration of the soy commodity chain raises questions about the narrative that influences soy production, and subsequently its increase over time. By examining the rise of soy production from 1990 onward, we find that a market- centric narrative instructs each aspect of soy, from economics, environment, labor and geography. If we consider soy production through the lens of neoliberalism—emphasizing the liberation of trade and output as a measure of productivity—we can begin to understand the rationale for increasing soy production as it relates to a market-centric understanding of the food system. With the dramatic soybean production, U.S. starts to turn itself to be a soybean exporter from a soybean consumer. In 2000, about one-third of the soybean was exported internationally. However, in 2015, the number increases to about 45%, which indicates the different role U.S plays in the soybean production chain and the impact of WTO on the international food trade. This relates to the idea of Food As Weapon. “With reforms in Mexico's domestic crop support programs, imports have virtually displaced domestic soybean production, with nearly all imports coming from the United States. U.S. soybean exports to Mexico have more than doubled since 1993.” (USDA) Due to the competitive price with subsidy and unblock of trade restriction (WTO), countries, such as Mexico, are going to totally depend on imported agricultural product, which impairs the domestic agricultural sector. And if other countries do join the soybean market, places like Brazil and Argentina see increased rates of deforestation and other ecological impacts that are mostly avoided in American production. After researching economics, labor, environment and geography, we see how each serves as a mechanism through which this food system narrative is realized. Commodity trading, changes in technology, and shifts in labor are some of the tools used to mobilize a market-centric goal: increasing yield. The primary assumption made around increasing yield is namely that there is a positive association between increasing output and growth. This assumption should be scrutinized, as it often leads to disproportionate access and systematically benefits some, and impoverishes others. Overall, this game of participating in lucrative markets only benefits the few who receive the profit. In the context of soy, we find that they are close relationships between a desired increase in yield with increased environmental degradation, harmful labor practices and strain on farmers whose livelihoods depend on volatile soy pricing.
