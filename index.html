Soy Commodity Chain Analysis

About This Project

Soybeans have quickly become one of the most important staple crops of the world. First cultivated in ancient East Asia, these oilseeds are now widely cultivated in many countries across the world, and soy is a components of countless food products. Our aim in this project is to examine multiple facets of the soy commodity chain in order to investigate the factors behind the rise of the soybean in recent decades. We have chosen four main angles for our investigation: geography, labor, economics, and ecology.

Where Does Soy Go?


Soy has become one of the most widely grown commodity crops - but how is it consumed?

Soybeans


Soybeans can be processed and used whole, but are primarily crushed to separate the bean into oil and protein-rich meal.

Whole Beans


Whole soybeans are sometimes processed into full-fat soy flour. Whole beans or full-fat soy flour is used in products like soymilk, tofu, and soy sauce. Immature beans may also be cooked and eaten.



Soybean Meal


Soybean meal is rich in protein, and an excellent animal feed. About 85% of the global soybean crop is processed into soybean meal and oil, and the bulk of the resulting meal is fed to livestock. Soybean meal can also be further processed into defatted soy flour.

Soybean Oil


Soybean oil is one of the top vegetable oils and ubiquitous in processed foods. It can also be processed into lecithin, a common emulsifier, among numerous other products.
