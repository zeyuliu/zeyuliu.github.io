Labor, Production, and Technology Analysis

How Soy is Used

Between the years of 1980-2011, soybean production increased 96% and yield increased 55%. Soy is has many uses, so part of this dramatic increase in production can be attributed to its increasing profitability leading to popularity among farmers. One 60 pound bushel of soy yields 48 pounds of protein-rich meal and 11 pounds of oil. 98% of U.S. soy becomes feed for pigs, chickens, and cows. 30 million metric tons of soy meal are consumed as feed each year. Soybeans are also a popular oilseed, accounting for 90% of oilseed production in the United States.

While popularity of the crop is partially responsible for the rise in production, the huge increases in yield are a result of technological advancements and new practices in soybean cultivation. These methods include increased planting flexibility, narrow rowed seeding practices, increased 50-50 soy and corn rotations, and lowered production cost due to adoption of herbicide resistant varieties of soy.

How Soy is Grown

Soy is planted in the months of May to June, and the crop is harvested September through October. Studies at the University of Wisconsin show that a corn-soy rotation of crops results in higher yields than the respective monocultures. To plant soy, land is tilled by a tractor. A planter deposits seeds 1.5 inches deep, in rows that average 30 inches apart. Sometimes, soybeans are “drilled” into the ground in 7-inch rows by a no-till planter. This technique makes use of the remains of the last crop to be planted in the field, which saves time, conserves moisture, and decreases soil erosion. This method drastically increases yield per acre, but is ineffective against tough weeds.
